Most Dubai brands we audit have the same Arabic content posture: either none at all, or a half-hearted machine-translated layer that performs worse than English-only would. The middle ground — Arabic content that actually moves the needle without requiring a full native content team — is where the opportunity is.

Here's the framework we use.

## Step 1: Don't translate everything. Translate the right things.

The instinct, when you decide to "do Arabic", is to mirror every English asset. This is expensive and usually unnecessary. The Pareto distribution of Arabic content value, in our experience:

- **Ad copy** — translating the headline and CTA gives you 60-70% of the ROAS lift from a full Arabic campaign. Don't translate the body if your headline lands.
- **Landing page headline + offer + CTA** — translate these three. Leave the rest in English if you have to.
- **Booking/checkout flow** — translate every button and form label. Friction in your conversion path is where Arabic-preference users drop off most heavily.
- **WhatsApp templates** — your auto-responder, your booking confirmation, your reminder. These produce trust at low cost.
- **Top 5 blog posts and pages by traffic** — eventually. Not on day one.

What you don't need to translate immediately: every blog post, your About page, your privacy policy, sub-pages with low traffic.

## Step 2: Use Arabic where the Arabic-preference user is most likely to be

Not every Arabic-preference user uses Arabic UI. Many UAE residents read Arabic fluently but operate phones in English. The signal you should use is intent context, not user language settings.

- A Dubai user searching "أفضل صالون تجميل" on Google is in Arabic mode. Serve them an Arabic landing page.
- The same user searching "best beauty salon Dubai" is in English mode. Serve English even if you know they're Arabic-fluent.
- A user clicking your Arabic Meta ad has self-selected. Send them to an Arabic landing page.
- A user clicking your English Meta ad — even if they're Arabic-speaking — wants English. Don't switch them.

This sounds obvious but most "bilingual" Dubai sites force a language at root and break this matching. The right pattern is: language follows the entry point, not the user.

## Step 3: Avoid machine translation for anything customer-facing

Google Translate and DeepL produce Arabic that reads obviously machine-generated to UAE residents. Common tells:

- Literal English idioms translated word-for-word ("at the end of the day" rendered literally)
- Misuse of definite articles (alif-lam) where context demands or forbids them
- Incorrect gender agreement on adjectives and verbs (Arabic verbs change with subject gender)
- Wrong dialect register — Modern Standard Arabic where Khaleeji dialect would land warmer

A bad Arabic translation actively damages brand trust. Better options:

- **Native Arabic copywriter on a per-piece basis** — usually AED 200-500 per ad set, AED 800-2,000 per landing page. Worth every dirham
- **Cultural review by a native speaker** of your machine-translated draft — cheaper but still requires real human input
- **Bilingual brand voice guide** — pay once for a native copywriter to write your brand voice in Arabic, then use it as reference for future content

## Step 4: Arabic creative needs Arabic aesthetic

This is where most Western agencies get it wrong. Translating the words isn't enough. Arabic content benefits from:

- **Visual rhythm that reads right-to-left** — the eye lands on different positions in an Arabic vs English layout, and creative composition should reflect that
- **Typography that respects the language** — using Latin fonts forced to do Arabic is jarring. Invest in proper Arabic typeface licensing (29LT, TPTQ, Khatt, Fontstand have good options for the UAE market)
- **Imagery that fits the audience** — for Khaleeji-targeting content, abaya-and-thobe wardrobing where appropriate, regional architecture, regional context cues

A landing page that has Arabic copy but Western-stock photography reads as inauthentic. The whole asset should belong to its language.

## Step 5: Measure Arabic separately

The biggest hidden problem with bilingual sites: most analytics setups blend Arabic and English performance into one funnel, hiding the fact that Arabic is significantly outperforming or underperforming.

What to do:

- Tag every Arabic ad campaign with `?lang=ar` UTM parameter
- Set up a custom dimension in GA4 for Arabic vs English landing pages
- Look at conversion rate by language column in your weekly P&L review
- A/B test Arabic vs English creative on the same product, same audience — Arabic wins 60-70% of the time in our tests for UAE-resident audiences

## Step 6: WhatsApp is where Arabic content matters most

WhatsApp is where Arabic-preference UAE users live. The single highest-ROI Arabic content investment for most Dubai brands is the WhatsApp Business flow:

- Welcome message in Arabic when they message in Arabic, English when in English (auto-detect via first message)
- All template messages (booking confirmations, reminders, follow-ups) localized
- A small library of Arabic FAQ answers a human agent can paste, instead of typing fresh each time

Spend the AED 2,000-3,000 to get this set up properly. The conversion difference vs an English-only WhatsApp flow is large.

## What good Arabic posture actually looks like

A Dubai brand with good Arabic posture has:

- Arabic ad copy on roughly half their Meta and TikTok creative
- Arabic landing pages for paid campaigns running in Arabic
- A WhatsApp flow that detects language and responds correctly
- A small Arabic-content backlog (5-10 blog posts, key service pages) translated by a native speaker
- Brand voice documented in Arabic, not just English

A brand without this is leaving conversion on the table. The investment to fix it is modest — usually AED 8,000-15,000 one-time setup, plus AED 2,000-4,000 monthly for ongoing creative — and produces immediate, measurable lift.
